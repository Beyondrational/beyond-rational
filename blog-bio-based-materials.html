What "bio-based"
actually means.

The label shows up on a lot of spec sheets right now. It doesn't always mean the same thing twice.

Beyond Rational · Copenhagen

"Bio-based" is a material-composition claim, not a sustainability score. It means some share of a product's mass comes from biological sources — wood, hemp, mycelium, straw — rather than fossil feedstock. That's a genuinely useful thing to know. It's also a much narrower claim than the word tends to imply on a mood board.

A product can be 100% bio-based by mass and still carry a meaningful carbon footprint, depending on how the raw material was grown, harvested, processed, and transported, and on what's used to bind it together. Conversely, a product with a smaller bio-based share can sometimes outperform a "fully" bio-based one on lifecycle emissions, if the processing is efficient and the transport distance is short. Composition and footprint are related, but they're not the same measurement.

What to actually check

Three things tell you more than the label alone: what the binder is (synthetic resins and adhesives can dominate a product's environmental impact even when the bulk fibre is natural), where GWP-fossil and GWP-biogenic land separately on the EPD (biogenic carbon storage is real, but it's a different number from avoided fossil emissions, and mixing the two overstates the win), and what happens at end of life (a bio-based product that can't be separated from its binder for recycling or composting just becomes a different kind of landfill mass).

None of this is a reason to distrust bio-based materials — it's a reason to read past the word. A tile made from foam-formed wood fibre with a mineral or cement-free binder, no synthetic flame retardants, and a documented recyclable end of life is a materially different product from one that happens to share the same headline claim. The spec sheet, not the label, is where that difference shows up.

Ask for the EPD, not the marketing copy

An Environmental Product Declaration is a standardised, third-party-verified document — it can't say things a brand's website can get away with. If a bio-based claim doesn't have an EPD backing it, that's usually the first thing worth asking about before it goes on the spec.
